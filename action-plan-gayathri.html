Bureaucratic Inertia: The Clock is Ticking on the Gayathri Report

By KABI ALUPENDRA | Investigative Desk Bengaluru Bureau
THE SILENT ASSEMBLY: The Karnataka Legislative Assembly, where the Gayathri Report remains "under review" fifty-three days after its submission. (Photo: TTT Advocacy Archive)

In the high-security corridors of the Vidhana Soudha, a 200-page document is currently gathering dust. Submitted on March 4, 2026, the Gayathri Report represents a scientific and legal masterpiece that details the pathway for the official recognition of Tulu. However, since its delivery, the state administration has maintained a strategic silence, failing to issue even a basic Action Taken Report (ATR) to the community leaders who commissioned the work.

Legal analysts suggest that this delay is no longer a technical matter of "vetting" or "translation." It has become an act of political evasion. Under standard parliamentary ethics, any report of this magnitude—affecting the cultural and administrative rights of millions—should have seen a Cabinet Note issued within the first twenty-one days. We are now entering Day 54 of a total information vacuum.

"This silence is an active erasure of the Tuluva administrative identity. We are no longer asking for recognition; we are demanding the release of the ATR."

The Stalling Tactics

The Tuluva Guardian's investigative team has tracked the document through three different departmental desks. As of last Tuesday, the report was reportedly sitting with the Department of Kannada and Culture, where sources say it is being treated as a 'regional concern' rather than a 'state priority.' This characterization ignores the fact that Tulu is one of the five primary Dravidian languages of India.

The implications of this stalling are far-reaching. Without official language status, Tuluva students are deprived of state-funded higher education resources, and coastal residents remain second-class citizens in their own administrative offices. The Gayathri Report was designed to bridge this gap by providing a legal framework for Article 345 implementation.

Pressure from the Coast

In Udupi and Mangaluru, the sentiment is shifting from patience to proactive agitation. The TTT (Tulu, Tulunad, Tuluvas) movement has already begun drafting legal notices to serve to the State Secretariat. If the ATR is not presented by the next legislative session, activists have signaled that a massive 'RTI Strike' will be launched across every taluk in Tulunad.
