Fifty Days of Bureaucratic Inertia: The Clock is Ticking on Tuluva Rights

By KABI ALUPENDRA | Investigative Desk Bengaluru Bureau
THE SILENT ASSEMBLY: The Karnataka Legislative Assembly, where the Gayathri Report (Submitted March 4) remains "Under Review" fifty-three days later. (Photo: TTT Advocacy Archive)

In the high-security corridors of the Vidhana Soudha, a 200-page document is currently gathering dust. Since its formal submission on March 4, 2026, the Gayathri Report—a comprehensive roadmap for Tulu's official status—has been met with strategic silence from the state administration. This "60-Day Silence" is now being viewed by legal analysts not as a technical delay, but as a political evasion.

The report, which details the linguistic, historical, and economic viability of Tulu as a second official language, was expected to trigger an immediate Action Taken Report (ATR). However, as we approach the two-month mark, no Cabinet Note has been circulated, and the Department of Kannada and Culture remains tight-lipped about the scheduling of a legislative debate.

"The government's refusal to table the ATR is an active erasure of Tuluva administrative identity. We are no longer asking; we are demanding the release of the report."

The Administrative Bottleneck
Our investigative desk has confirmed that the report has passed through the initial vetting stage but is currently stalled at the Secretariat level. Sources suggest that the administration is wary of the precedent it sets for other linguistic movements, ignoring the unique "Classical" status and historical court-language pedigree that Tulu holds.

For the Tuluva community, this isn't just about a name on a paper; it's about administrative access. Without official status, Tuluva citizens are often treated as strangers in their own government offices. The Gayathri Report provides the legal "key" to fix this, yet the door remains locked from the inside.

The Rising Coast
From Udupi to Kasaragod, the sentiment is shifting. The TTT (Tulu, Tulunad, Tuluvas) movement is preparing for a massive RTI-driven accountability phase. If the ATR is not presented by the next session, activists plan to flood the Secretariat with thousands of individual inquiries, forcing a response through sheer volume.

It has been fifty-three days since the Gayathri Report was formally handed to the Secretariat in Bengaluru. For the uninitiated, this 200-page document represents more than just a linguistic petition; it is a meticulously researched blueprint for the constitutional recognition of Tulu under Article 345. Yet, as of April 26, 2026, the document remains "under review"—a bureaucratic euphemism for strategic shelving. Our investigation into the Department of Kannada and Culture reveals a disturbing pattern of procedural stagnation. Sources within the Vidhana Soudha, speaking on the condition of anonymity, suggest that the file has not moved past the secondary vetting stage in over three weeks. This is not merely a technical delay; it is a calculated effort to dampen the momentum of the Tuluva sovereignty movement.

The legal framework for state language recognition is clear. Once a state-appointed committee submits its findings, the government is mandated to present an Action Taken Report (ATR) during the subsequent legislative session. By withholding the Gayathri Report from the floor of the House, the administration is effectively bypassing the democratic rights of millions of Tulu speakers. The "60-Day Silence" is becoming a rallying cry for the TTT (Tulu, Tulunad, Tuluvas) movement, signaling a shift from polite advocacy to aggressive legal accountability.

"The government's refusal to table the ATR is an active erasure of Tuluva administrative identity. We are no longer asking; we are reclaiming a sovereignty that dates back to the Alupa kings."

Central to the Gayathri Report is the Pelattur stone inscription, a 7th-century artifact that serves as the "Magna Carta" of Tuluva history. Paleographic analysis of the inscription confirms that Tulu was the primary administrative and court language of the early Alupa dynasty. This historical pedigree is vital because it debunks the myth that Tulu is merely a "spoken dialect." It was a language of statecraft, law, and commerce long before the modern linguistic reorganization of India. The report argues that restoring Tulu to its official status is not a "new" demand, but a restoration of a historical right that was eroded during colonial and post-colonial administrative shifts.

Compounding this historical evidence is the recent technical breakthrough in digital sovereignty. With the Tulu-Tigalari script now integrated into Unicode 16.0, the "technical impossibility" argument often used by bureaucrats has been dismantled. The development of independent Android keyboard apps and standardized digital fonts means that Tulu can now function seamlessly in modern governance, from digital certificates to official government portals. The Gayathri Report provides the technical roadmap for this transition, yet the political will remains frozen in an era of linguistic mono-culture.

The economic cost of this delay is staggering. Linguistic exclusion is, at its core, an economic barrier. In the coastal districts of Udupi and Dakshina Kannada, the lack of official status for Tulu means that state-funded educational resources, academic chairs, and cultural grants are diverted elsewhere. Students in these regions are deprived of the opportunity to study their mother tongue at an advanced academic level with government backing, a right enjoyed by speakers of recognized languages under the Eighth Schedule. Furthermore, the "Tulu Kadal" (Tulu Sea) concept explored in the report highlights the potential for heritage-based maritime tourism. By refusing to recognize the Tuluva identity, the state is effectively capping the economic potential of the coastal belt.

Our investigation also points to a "Representation Gap." In government offices across Tulunad, official documents and public notices are rarely available in the language spoken by the majority of the residents. This creates a disconnect between the state and its citizens, leading to administrative inefficiency and a sense of alienation among the youth. The Gayathri Report proposes a dual-language administrative model, similar to the one used successfully in other multi-linguistic states, which would bridge this gap and foster local economic growth.

In response to this silence, the TTT movement has activated its "Strategic Accountability Phase." The directive is clear: every Tuluva citizen is now a monitor of the state. The movement has introduced a two-strike system to force the government’s hand. Strike 1 involves the mass-filing of Digital RTIs. By flooding the Department of Kannada and Culture with specific queries regarding the "Note Files" of the Gayathri Report, activists are creating a legal paper trail that cannot be ignored. Strike 2 involves localized political pressure. MLAs from the coastal belt are being put on notice; their silence on the floor of the Assembly will be viewed as a betrayal of their constituency.

The sentiment on the ground in Udupi and Mangaluru is shifting. The era of quiet petitions is over. The Tuluva Guardian will continue to provide the templates, the links, and the investigative depth required to sustain this movement. We are documenting every day of this delay, and we will hold the individuals responsible for this bureaucratic inertia accountable in the court of public opinion. The countdown to the 60-day mark is nearly over, and the response from the people of Tulunad will be unprecedented.
