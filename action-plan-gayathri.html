GOVERNANCE

Fifty Days of Bureaucratic Inertia: The Fate of the Gayathri Report

By KABI ALUPENDRA | INVESTIGATIVE DESK
The Karnataka Legislative Assembly in Bengaluru, where the Gayathri Report remains pending since its formal submission on March 4, 2026. (Photo: TTT Archive)

In the corridors of the Vidhana Soudha, silence has become a policy. It has been fifty-three days since the Gayathri Commission, a high-level body tasked with analyzing the linguistic viability of Tulu, submitted its exhaustive findings to the State Government. While the Tuluva community anticipated a swift transition to legislative debate, the report has seemingly entered the nebulous "vetting" phase—a stage often used by administrations to delay politically sensitive decisions.

The report, submitted on March 4, is not merely a collection of suggestions. It is a scientific codification of the 7th-century administrative history of Tulunad, the structural integrity of the Tulu-Tigalari script, and the modern economic necessity of linguistic recognition. Legal experts argue that the delay in tabling the Action Taken Report (ATR) borders on a breach of administrative ethics.

"The government's refusal to acknowledge the March 4 submission is not just a delay; it is an active erasure of the Tuluva administrative identity."

A History of Delay

This is not the first time Tuluva aspirations have been met with a 'wait-and-see' approach. However, the Gayathri Report represents a significant milestone—it is the first comprehensive legal document that addresses Article 345 requirements with modern linguistic data. Critics within the TTT (Tulu, Tulunad, Tuluvas) movement point out that similar reports in other states have seen cabinet sub-committees formed within 14 days. Here, we are approaching the two-month mark with zero official correspondence.

The Administrative Vacuum

Why does the ATR matter? In parliamentary democracy, the ATR is the bridge between a commission's ideas and a law's reality. Without it, the Gayathri Report is just paper. The Tuluva Guardian's investigative desk has found that no Cabinet Note has been circulated regarding this report as of this week, indicating that the Department of Kannada and Culture is currently the primary bottleneck.

As the summer session of the Assembly approaches, the pressure on coastal MLAs is mounting. The common Tuluva is no longer satisfied with oral promises; the demand is for the report to be tabled, debated, and passed into law.

HISTORICAL CONTEXT

From the Alupa inscriptions to the Pelattur records, Tulu was a language of the court for 1,000 years. The Gayathri Report simply asks for a return to this status.

ECONOMIC IMPACT

Official status would unlock central funding for Tulu research, inclusion in the 8th Schedule, and localized governance efficiency for over 4 million people.

BEYOND THE COAST

This is not just a regional issue. It is a test for India’s linguistic federalism. Can a classical language survive in a modern state bureaucracy?
