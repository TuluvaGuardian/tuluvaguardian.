Investigation: Part I

Fifty Days of Bureaucratic Inertia: The Clock is Ticking on Tuluva Rights

BY KABI ALUPENDRA | Investigative Desk | Bengaluru Bureau
THE SILENT ASSEMBLY: The Karnataka Legislative Assembly, where the Gayathri Report remains "under review" fifty-three days after its formal handover to the State Government.

In the high-security corridors of the Vidhana Soudha, a 200-page document is currently gathering dust. Since its formal submission on March 4, 2026, the Gayathri Report—a comprehensive roadmap for Tulu's official status—has been met with strategic silence from the state administration. This "60-Day Silence" is now being characterized by legal analysts and TTT (Tulu, Tulunad, Tuluvas) activists not merely as a technical delay, but as a deliberate act of political evasion. Under standard parliamentary ethics, a report of this magnitude, affecting the administrative rights of millions, should have seen a Cabinet Note issued within the first twenty-one days.

Our investigative desk has confirmed through high-level sources in the Secretariat that the report has passed the initial linguistic vetting stage. However, it is currently "parked" in a department-level bottleneck. Sources suggest that the administration is wary of the precedent it sets for other linguistic movements, choosing to ignore the unique "Classical" pedigree and historical court-language status that Tulu holds. By remaining silent, the administration is bypassing its constitutional duty to present an Action Taken Report (ATR) to the people of Tulunad.

"The government's refusal to acknowledge the March 4 submission is not just a delay; it is an active erasure of the Tuluva administrative identity. We are no longer asking; we are demanding the release of the ATR."

The core of the Gayathri Report rests on historical sovereignty. Central to this argument is the Pelattur stone inscription. Dating back to the 7th century, this artifact is the "smoking gun" of Tuluva history. Our researchers have analyzed high-resolution photographic evidence which confirms that Tulu was not merely a spoken dialect, but a formal administrative tongue of the early Alupa kings. This establishes a 1,400-year lineage of court usage that precedes the formation of modern state borders by a millennium. To ignore this evidence is to deny the historical legitimacy of one of India's oldest administrative traditions.

Furthermore, the digital evolution of the script marks a turning point. With the Tulu-Tigalari script being integrated into Unicode 16.0, the technical barriers for official use have vanished. The development of an independent Android keyboard and standardized digital fonts means that the state can no longer cite "lack of technical infrastructure" as a reason for delay. Digital sovereignty is now a reality, yet the political will remains frozen in time.

The exclusion of Tulu from official state status has direct fiscal and social consequences. For the thousands of students in coastal Karnataka, the lack of linguistic recognition translates to a lack of state-funded educational resources and specialized academic chairs. In government offices in Udupi and Mangaluru, residents are often treated as "strangers" because the primary language of the land holds no legal weight in the filing of documents or the execution of local laws.

Economically, the "Tulu Sea" (Tulu Kadal) concept explored in the report highlights the ancient maritime trade routes connecting this region to civilizations in Africa and the Middle East. Recognition would open doors for cultural tourism and heritage funding that are currently locked. The Gayathri Report provides the legal "key" to unlock this potential, yet the door remains barred from the inside by a bureaucracy that fears coastal autonomy.

As the 60-day mark approaches, the TTT movement is shifting its strategy from advocacy to legal confrontation. The movement has identified a "Strike System" to force accountability. Strike 1 involves the massive deployment of Digital RTIs targeted at the Department of Kannada and Culture. These RTIs are designed to force the release of "Note Files" which will reveal exactly which officials are stalling the report. Strike 2 involves localized MLA pressure, demanding that representatives raise a "Point of Order" in the upcoming legislative session.

The sentiment from Udupi to Kasaragod is clear: the era of silent waiting is over. The Tuluva Guardian will continue to monitor the status of the Gayathri Report daily. We will name the departments responsible for delays and provide our readers with the exact tools needed to challenge this bureaucratic inertia. The countdown to the ATR presentation has begun, and the people of Tulunad are watching.
