Historical Narrative

The Pelattur Incident: A 700 AD Legal Thriller

It was a Sunday morning in the 7th Century, and the coastal village of Pelattur was in turmoil. A high-ranking nobleman, Varandeva Perbappena Bamanna Pervarasa, stood accused of a crime so severe it threatened the peace of the Alupa Kingdom. This wasn't a matter for a village elder; it required the full weight of the state.

The King (Arasu) didn't act alone. He summoned the Council of 24—a group of elected or appointed representatives from the Kilattur region. Leading the proceedings was the Chief Officer Uruppana, the king's most trusted executor, whose name was already feared across the coastal belt from Vaddarse to Belman.

The Act of Appointment (Nēmakathi)

To ensure the trial was fair and legally binding, the inscription records the Tatthu (appointment/recruitment) of these 24 men. In our modern language, this was a formal Nēmakathi (ನೇಮಕಾತಿ). These were not just bystanders; they were the jury and the judges of the Alupa state.

The Verdict and the Eternal Curse

The trial ended in a guilty verdict. The crime was recorded in the Tulu-Tigalari script so that no one in the future could claim ignorance. But a simple fine wasn't enough for a man of Pervarasa's status. The inscription concludes with a chilling warning:

"Whoever violates this decree, or protects the accused, shall carry the sin of slaying a thousand tawny cows on the banks of the Varanasi."

Why this Story Matters

This isn't just about a crime; it's about Sophistication. It tells us that:

  • Tulu was the Language of Command: The law was written in Tulu so the common people and the 24 council members could understand it.
  • Power was Balanced: Even a 'Pervarasa' (Great King/Officer) was answerable to the 'Council of 24'.
  • Continuity: The word Tatthu reminds us that our modern Nēmakathi (recruitment) has roots stretching back over a millennium.
