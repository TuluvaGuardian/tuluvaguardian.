Historical Discovery

The Pelattur Inscription: Evidence of a 7th-Century Tuluva Parliament

Over 1,300 years ago, the coastal landscape of the Alupa Kingdom was not ruled by the whims of a single man, but by the rule of law. The Pelattur inscription tells the story of a high-stakes trial. A nobleman named Varandeva Perbappena Bamanna Pervarasa stood accused of a grave crime against the state.

To deliver justice, the King (Arasu) and his Chief Officer Uruppana did not simply pass a sentence. They convened the Iruvatta Naluveren—the Council of 24—a legislative and judicial assembly that functioned as a proto-parliament for the Tuluva people.

The Iruvatta Naluveren: A Legacy of Democracy

While much of the world was under absolute monarchies, the Alupas established a system of Decentralized Governance. The Council of 24 represented local interests and ensured that "Justice" was a collective Tuluva responsibility. This assembly is the historical ancestor of our modern local bodies.

Linguistic Continuity: The Recruitment (Nēmakathi)

The inscription specifically mentions the Tatthu (selection/positioning) of these council members. This provides a direct link to our modern administrative vocabulary:

7th Century Archaic Term Modern Tulu (Administrative) Historical Context
Tatthu Nēmakathi (ನೇಮಕಾತಿ) The formal appointment or recruitment of officials.
Iruvatta Naluveren Pratinidhi Mandali The "Council of 24"—the Tuluva Parliament.
Adikari / Uruppana Karyanirvahaka The executive signature that validated the law.

The Verdict: A Language of Command

The story ends with a sentence etched in stone—a warning that anyone who interfered with this parliamentary decision would face the ultimate spiritual penalty. Because this was recorded in the native script, we know that Tulu was the Language of the Public Record.

"Tulu was not just spoken in the fields; it was spoken in the Parliament. From the Alupa courts to the modern Gboard—the script remains our voice."
