The Shudra Stigma: How Caste Myths, Elite Neglect, and Buried History Silenced Tulu

By Tuluva Guardian Investigations | May 2026
Prologue: When a Civilization Is Told Its Language Is “Low”
Not every language is conquered by armies. Some are conquered by ideas. Some are pushed to the margins not by invaders, but by scholars, bureaucrats, social hierarchies, and even their own educated elites.

That is the story of Tulu. One of South Asia’s oldest living languages… spoken across the sacred coast of Tulunadu… carrying oral epics, maritime memory, spirit traditions, agricultural science, and a linguistic structure older than many recognized literary traditions.

And yet, for nearly two centuries, Tulu was made to wear a social label: “Shudra Bhasha.” The language of cultivators. The language of fishermen. The language of artisans. The language of others.

"But history has a habit of resisting false narratives. And in the case of Tulu—history was carved in stone."

I. The Manufacture of a Stigma

When Language Became Caste

During the 19th and early 20th centuries, under colonial classification and upper-caste linguistic politics, languages were not judged merely by grammar or antiquity. They were judged by who spoke them.

Tulu—despite its vast oral literature, ritual vocabulary, and independent linguistic structure—was increasingly reduced to a “coarse village tongue.” This was not linguistics. This was caste politics wearing academic clothing.

The Silence of the Elites

Influence of figures like Benegal Narsing Rau and Manjeshwar Govinda Pai: Both emerged from Tulu soil, yet their public energy largely strengthened larger prestige traditions—Kannada, Sanskrit, and pan-Indian literary culture.

II. The Stone That Shattered the Myth

Pelatur: The Inscription That Changed Everything

Then, history spoke. Not through folklore, but through stone. The discovery of the Pelatur Inscription by M. Rajagopalakrishna Rao fundamentally altered the Tulu historical debate.

Dating to the 7th century CE, this inscription pushed written Tulu back nearly 1,300 years. It proved something the “Shudra language” narrative could never explain: Tulu was not merely spoken in fields; Tulu was carved in royal stone.

III. The First Rebel: S.U. Paniyadi

In 1928, Srinivasa Upadhyaya Paniyadi organized the Tulu Mahasabha. This was not a cultural festival; it was intellectual rebellion. Paniyadi understood a simple truth: If a language has grammar—it cannot be dismissed as primitive.

IV. The Intellectual Revolution

The story of Tulu’s reclamation is built on the shoulders of giants:

  • Dr. Palthady Puncithaya: Who proved folklore was civilizational data.
  • P.S. Rai: The architect of lexical sovereignty.
  • Ravindra Mundkur: Who refused to see Tulunadu as a district and saw it as a global civilization.
  • Bannanje Govindacharya: The Sanskritic scholar who declared Tulu as a classical language, collapsing the old hierarchy.
"Tulu was not folk language. This was administrative language. Court language. Elite language. State language."

Epilogue: The Language They Called “Shudra”

They said Tulu was the language of the Shudra. History now answers: If by Shudra you mean the builders of ports, the keepers of land records, the navigators of oceans, and the rulers of the Alupa Dynasty—Then yes. And India owes that language justice.
