The Shudra Stigma: How Caste Myths, Elite Neglect, and Buried History Silenced Tulu

By Tuluva Guardian Investigations | May 2026
Prologue: When a Civilization Is Told Its Language Is “Low”
Not every language is conquered by armies. Some are conquered by ideas.

That is the story of Tulu. And yet, for nearly two centuries, Tulu was made to wear a social label: “Shudra Bhasha.”

I. The Manufacture of a Stigma: The Post-Keladi Erasure

The transition of Tulu from a language of power to a "marginal" tongue follows a specific historical arc. Following the fall of the Keladi Nayakas, who maintained a complex regional administration, a new socio-political elite emerged under colonial and neighboring linguistic influences.

These elites, seeking prestige in the colonial administrative structure, began to view Tulu through a lens of hierarchy. While the Keladi rule recognized the functional diversity of the coast, the subsequent eras saw Tulu rebranded specifically as a "Shudra tongue"—a language fit only for the fields, the Bhuta Kola, and the matrilineal homestead, but never the courtroom or the assembly.

II. The Stone That Shattered the Myth

The discovery of the Pelatur Inscription fundamentally destroyed the "Shudra language" myth. It proved that in the 7th century, under the Alupas, Tulu was carved in royal stone. It was a language of statecraft long before the post-Keladi elites sought to silence it.

III. The First Rebel: S.U. Paniyadi

In 1928, S.U. Paniyadi launched an intellectual rebellion. He authored the first formal Tulu grammar, the Tulu Vyakarana, proving that our tongue was not a "dialect" but a structured, independent language with its own internal architecture. He understood that without a book of rules, the elite would always dismiss Tulu as primitive. His work was the first wall of defense against linguistic erasure.

IV. The Intellectual Revolution

The Guardians of Civilizational Data

Following Paniyadi, a new generation of scholars moved from sentiment to science. Dr. Palthady Puncithaya spent a lifetime proving that the Paddanas (oral epics) were not just folklore, but a sophisticated record of Tuluva law, spirit traditions, and social history. He transformed oral memory into academic evidence.

Meanwhile, Ravindra Mundkur expanded the scope to the world stage. Through his investigations into maritime history and migration, he proved that Tulunadu was never a secluded district, but a global maritime power. His research into toponymy (village names) provided the linguistic fossils needed to prove Tulu’s antiquity pre-dates its more "prestigious" neighbors.

P.S. Rai completed this revolution by building the lexical fortress. His dictionaries proved that Tulu’s core vocabulary—especially for the ocean and the soil—was entirely original, shattering the myth that it was a "derivative" language.

IV. The Intellectual Revolution

The Guardians of Civilizational Data

Following Paniyadi, Dr. Palthady Puncithaya achieved a historic breakthrough by identifying the chronological "Six Pillars" of Tulu classical literature. These works, spanning the 11th to 17th centuries, provide the ultimate evidence that Tulu was never merely an oral dialect, but a language of kings, poets, and high-art.

The Tulu Classical Canon
  1. Devi Mahatme – Composed by the Adi Kavi (The First Poet) of Tulu.
  2. Tulu Mahabharato – The monumental 14th-century translation by Arunabja.
  3. Karnaparvo – Authored by King Harihara, proving royal Tuluva literacy.
  4. Sri Bhagavatho – The 17th-century sophisticated epic by Vishnutunga.
  5. Kaveri – A masterpiece of classical Tulu poetic architecture and aesthetics.
  6. Tulu Ramayana – The decisive epic record that completes the Tuluva Classical Triad.

By documenting these specific works, Puncithaya proved that while the post-Keladi elites sought to silence Tulu as a "village tongue," they were ignoring a 700-year-old literary legacy that matched any other classical language in India.
