The Shudra Stigma: How Caste Myths, Elite Neglect, and Buried History Silenced Tulu

By Tuluva Guardian Investigations | May 2026
Prologue: When a Civilization Is Told Its Language Is “Low”
Not every language is conquered by armies. Some are conquered by ideas.

That is the story of Tulu. For nearly two centuries, Tulu was made to wear a social label: “Shudra Bhasha.”

I. The Manufacture of a Stigma: The Post-Keladi Erasure

Following the fall of the Keladi Nayakas, a new socio-political elite emerged. Seeking prestige in colonial structures, they began to view Tulu through a lens of hierarchy. Tulu was rebranded as a "Shudra tongue"—a language fit only for the fields and homesteads, never the courtroom.

II. The Stone That Shattered the Myth

The Pelatur Inscription proved that in the 7th century, under the Alupas, Tulu was carved in royal stone. It was a language of statecraft long before the post-Keladi elites sought to silence it.

    III. The First Rebel: S.U. Paniyadi

    The Architect of Tulu Sovereignty

    In 1928, while the post-Keladi elites were busy perfecting their Kannada and English to suit colonial tastes, Srinivasa Upadhyaya Paniyadi did the unthinkable: he declared that Tulu did not need a "parent" language. By organizing the Tulu Mahasabha, he shifted the conversation from mere culture to hard-nosed politics.

    The First Grammar

    Paniyadi authored the Tulu Vyakarana, providing the mathematical proof that Tulu possessed a sophisticated, independent internal logic that required no external validation.

    The Script Advocacy

    He was the earliest modern voice to demand the reclamation of the Tulu-Tigalari script, refusing to let the language be "borrowed" or buried by other writing systems.

    "If a language is spoken by millions and possesses its own verbs, tenses, and syntax, its exclusion from the state is not a 'lack of merit'—it is a deliberate silencing."

    Paniyadi’s rebellion was the first modern crack in the "Shudra Stigma." He proved that Tulu had architecture, discipline, and a right to the assembly. He paved the way for the intellectual revolution that would follow decades later.

The label of "Shudra Bhasha" was never a linguistic fact; it was an administrative weapon. By branding Tulu as a language of the fields, the post-Keladi elites and colonial-era bureaucrats successfully kept it out of the Eighth Schedule for over 70 years.
